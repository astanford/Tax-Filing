## Rules

### 1. What Can Be Depreciated — Building Yes, Land NO

- > "You can't depreciate the cost of land because land generally doesn't wear out, become obsolete, or get used up. But if it does, the loss is accounted for upon disposition. The costs of clearing, grading, planting, and landscaping are usually all part of the cost of land and can't be depreciated." [Pub 527 (2025), p. 9]
- Equipment used to build capital improvements, and property placed in service and disposed of in the same year, also can't be depreciated. [Pub 527 (2025), p. 9]
- **Allocating purchase price between land and building:** "If you buy buildings and your cost includes the cost of the land on which they stand, you must divide the cost between the land and the buildings to figure the basis for depreciation of the buildings. The part of the cost that you allocate to each asset is the ratio of the FMV of that asset to the FMV of the whole property at the time you buy it. **If you aren't certain of the FMVs of the land and the buildings, you can divide the cost between them based on their assessed values for real estate tax purposes.**" [Pub 527 (2025), p. 11]
- Pub 527's example: house + land bought for $200,000; tax assessment showed $136,000 house / $24,000 land of a $160,000 assessed value → allocate 85% ($136,000 ÷ $160,000) to the house and 15% to the land → depreciable basis $170,000 house, $30,000 land (not depreciable). [Pub 527 (2025), p. 11]
- Depreciation begins when the property is "ready and available" for rent (placed in service), even if not yet rented; it continues while the property is temporarily idle (e.g., between tenants). [Pub 527 (2025), p. 9]

### 2. Basis: Cost + Certain Closing Costs + Improvements

- Basis is generally cost: cash plus debt obligations (including an assumed mortgage), plus sales tax (unless deducted on Schedule A), freight, and installation/testing charges. [Pub 527 (2025), p. 10]
- **Settlement fees ADDED to basis:** "The following settlement fees and closing costs for buying the property are part of your basis in the property: Abstract fees. Charges for installing utility services. Legal fees. Recording fees. Surveys. Transfer taxes. Title insurance. Any amounts the seller owes that you agree to pay, such as back taxes or interest, recording or mortgage fees, charges for improvements or repairs, and sales commissions." [Pub 527 (2025), pp. 10–11]
- Seller's unreimbursed real estate taxes that you agree to pay are also added to basis (not deductible as taxes). [Pub 527 (2025), p. 10]
- **Settlement costs NOT added to basis:** fire insurance premiums; rent/occupancy charges before closing; loan-related charges — points (discount points, loan origination fees), loan assumption fees, credit report cost, lender-required appraisal fees; and escrow amounts for future taxes and insurance. [Pub 527 (2025), p. 11]
- **Increases to basis:** improvements/additions with a useful life over 1 year, casualty restoration costs, cost of extending utility lines, legal fees to defend/perfect title. Improvements made after the property is in service are depreciated as **separate property items**, using the class and recovery period that would apply to the original property (e.g., an addition to a rental house = 27.5-year residential rental property). [Pub 527 (2025), pp. 11, 13]
- **Decreases to basis** include insurance/casualty reimbursements, easement payments, any special depreciation allowance or §179 deduction claimed, and "depreciation you deducted or could have deducted." [Pub 527 (2025), p. 12]
- Property converted from personal use to rental: depreciable basis is the **lesser of adjusted basis or FMV** at the date of conversion. [Pub 527 (2025), p. 10]

### 3. Residential Rental Property = 27.5-Year Straight Line, Mid-Month (GDS) — and the Transient-Basis Exclusion

- "Generally, you must use the Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System (MACRS) to depreciate residential rental property placed in service after 1986." MACRS has two systems, GDS and ADS; "You must use GDS unless you are specifically required by law to use ADS or you elect to use ADS." [Pub 527 (2025), pp. 10, 12]
- "You must use the straight line method and a mid-month convention for residential rental property. In the first year that you claim depreciation for residential rental property, you can claim depreciation only for the number of months the property is in use." [Pub 527 (2025), p. 15]
- GDS recovery period: **27.5 years** for "Residential rental property (buildings or structures) and structural components such as furnaces, waterpipes, venting, etc." (ADS: 30 years). [Pub 527 (2025), Table 2-1, p. 14; Pub 946 (2025), Table 4-1, p. 33]
- **Definition (Pub 946):** "Residential rental property. This is any building or structure, such as a rental home (including a mobile home), if 80% or more of its gross rental income for the tax year is from dwelling units. A dwelling unit is a house or apartment used to provide living accommodations in a building or structure. **It does not include a unit in a hotel, motel, or other establishment where more than half the units are used on a transient basis.** If you occupy any part of the building or structure for personal use, its gross rental income includes the fair rental value of the part you occupy." [Pub 946 (2025), p. 30]
- **Definition (Pub 527):** "Residential rental property. This class includes any real property that is a rental building or structure (including a mobile home) for which 80% or more of the gross rental income for the tax year is from dwelling units. **It doesn't include a unit in a hotel, motel, inn, or other establishment where more than half of the units are used on a transient basis.** If you live in any part of the building or structure, the gross rental income includes the fair rental value of the part you live in." [Pub 527 (2025), p. 13]
- **If the property fails this definition, it is nonresidential real property:** "Nonresidential real property. This is section 1250 property, such as an office building, store, or warehouse, that is neither residential rental property nor property with a class life of less than 27.5 years." GDS recovery period: **39 years**, straight line, mid-month convention. [Pub 946 (2025), p. 30; Table 4-1, p. 33; Table A-7a, p. 74]
- **Short-term rental implication:** a unit rented hotel/motel-style on a transient basis (in an establishment where more than half the units are so used) is NOT residential rental property — it must be depreciated over **39 years** as nonresidential real property, not 27.5 years. The publications do not define a specific number of days for "transient" — I cannot verify a day-count threshold from these sources — check IRS.gov. [Pub 946 (2025), p. 30; Pub 527 (2025), p. 13]
- Mid-month convention: "you treat all property placed in service, or disposed of, during any month as placed in service, or disposed of, at the midpoint of that month." It applies to **all** residential rental and nonresidential real property. [Pub 527 (2025), p. 14]
- Optional ADS election (30-year SL for residential rental) is made in the first year, is irrevocable, and applies property-by-property for real property. [Pub 527 (2025), p. 13]

### 5. 5-, 7-, and 15-Year Property in Rental Activities

From Pub 527's GDS class list and Table 2-1 [Pub 527 (2025), p. 13; Table 2-1, p. 14]:

| GDS class / period | Rental assets included | Method/convention |
|---|---|---|
| 5-year | Computers and peripherals; office machinery; automobiles; light trucks; **"appliances, carpeting, and furniture used in a residential rental real estate activity"** (e.g., stoves, refrigerators) | 200% DB, half-year (or mid-quarter) [Pub 527 (2025), p. 15] |
| 7-year | Office furniture and equipment (desks, files); any property with no class life not designated elsewhere | 200% DB, half-year (or mid-quarter) [Pub 527 (2025), p. 15] |
| 15-year | **Roads, fences, and shrubbery (if depreciable)** — land improvements | 150% DB, half-year (or mid-quarter) [Pub 527 (2025), p. 15] |

- Year-1 half-year-convention table rates: 5-year = 20.00%, 7-year = 14.29%, 15-year = 5.00%. Mid-quarter convention is required if more than 40% of the basis of such property (excluding real property) is placed in service in the last 3 months of the year. [Pub 527 (2025), Table 2-2a–c, p. 16; p. 14]

### 6. Bonus Depreciation (§168(k)) and Section 179 for Rental Assets

**Special depreciation allowance (bonus):**
- "P.L. 119-21, commonly known as the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, reinstated the **100% special depreciation allowance** for certain qualified property acquired and placed in service **after January 19, 2025**" (an election to take 40% instead is available for the first tax year ending after that date). Property acquired **before January 20, 2025** and placed in service after December 31, 2024 and before January 1, 2026 is limited to a **40%** allowance under the phase-down rules (60% for long-production-period property and certain aircraft). [Pub 946 (2025), What's New, p. 1; Pub 527 (2025), What's New, p. 1]
- Qualified property includes "Tangible property depreciated under MACRS with a **recovery period of 20 years or less**." [Pub 946 (2025), p. 23] → For rentals, 5-year appliances/carpets/furniture, 7-year property, and 15-year land improvements can qualify; the 27.5-year building (and 39-year nonresidential building) and its structural components cannot, because their recovery period exceeds 20 years.
- "For 2025, some properties used in connection with residential real property activities may qualify for a special depreciation allowance. This allowance is figured before you figure your regular depreciation deduction." Electing OUT requires a statement attached to the return (see Form 4562 line 14 instructions). [Pub 527 (2025), p. 12]
- The allowance (and any §179 deduction) reduces basis before applying the MACRS table percentages. [Pub 527 (2025), pp. 12, 15]

**Section 179:**
- 2025 dollar limits: maximum deduction **$2,500,000**, reduced dollar-for-dollar by §179 property placed in service over **$4,000,000** (SUV limit $31,300). [Pub 946 (2025), What's New, p. 1; Pub 527 (2025), What's New, p. 1]
- **Trade-or-business requirement:** "To qualify for the section 179 deduction, your property must have been acquired for use in your trade or business. Property you acquire only for the production of income, such as investment property, **rental property (if renting property is not your trade or business)**, and property that produces royalties, does not qualify." [Pub 946 (2025), p. 16] → §179 is generally unavailable for a typical investment-level residential rental; it can apply only if the rental activity rises to a trade or business.
- Eligible tangible personal property includes "Certain property used predominantly to furnish lodging or in connection with the furnishing of lodging (except as provided in section 50(b)(2))." [Pub 946 (2025), p. 16; 2025 Instructions for Form 4562, p. 2] — so residential rental furnishings are no longer categorically excluded as lodging property, but the trade-or-business requirement above still applies.
- "Qualified section 179 real property" is limited to qualified improvement property (an improvement to an interior portion of a building that is **nonresidential** real property) and roofs, HVAC, fire protection/alarm, and security systems for **nonresidential** real property. The residential rental building itself and improvements to it are NOT eligible for §179. [Pub 946 (2025), p. 16]

### 7. "Allowed or Allowable" Rule

- > "You must reduce the basis of property by the depreciation **allowed or allowable, whichever is greater**. Depreciation allowed is depreciation you actually deducted (from which you received a tax benefit). Depreciation allowable is depreciation you are entitled to deduct. If you do not claim depreciation you are entitled to deduct, you must still reduce the basis of the property by the full amount of depreciation allowable." [Pub 946 (2025), p. 12]
- Pub 527 repeats it: "If you didn't claim all the depreciation you were entitled to deduct, you must still reduce your basis in the property by the full amount of depreciation that you could have deducted." If you deducted an incorrect amount, you may correct by Form 1040-X or by an accounting method change. [Pub 527 (2025), p. 17]
- Yearly depreciation deductions for the "basis fully recovered" stopping rule "include any depreciation that you were allowed to claim, even if you didn't claim it." [Pub 527 (2025), p. 9]

### 8. When Form 4562 Must Be Filed

Per "Who Must File": complete and file Form 4562 if you are claiming any of the following [2025 Instructions for Form 4562, p. 2]:
- Depreciation for property **placed in service during the 2025 tax year**;
- A **section 179** expense deduction (including a carryover);
- Depreciation on any **vehicle or other listed property** (regardless of when placed in service);
- A deduction for any vehicle reported on a form other than Schedule C;
- Any depreciation on a corporate return other than Form 1120-S;
- **Amortization** of costs that begins during the 2025 tax year.

Pub 527's rental-specific version: "You must complete and attach Form 4562 if you are claiming the following depreciation in your rental activity: Depreciation, including the special depreciation allowance, on property placed in service during 2025. Depreciation on listed property (such as a car), regardless of when it was placed in service." Otherwise (no new assets, no listed property, no §179, no new amortization), figure depreciation on your own worksheet — no Form 4562 needed, but keep the computations in your records. File a separate Form 4562 for each business or activity that requires one. [Pub 527 (2025), p. 18; 2025 Instructions for Form 4562, p. 2]

### 9. Depreciation Recapture at Sale

- **MACRS recapture:** "If you later dispose of property you depreciated using MACRS, any gain on the disposition is generally recaptured (included in income) as ordinary income up to the amount of the depreciation previously allowed or allowable for the property." [2025 Instructions for Form 4562, p. 13]
- **Special depreciation allowance recapture:** gain on disposition is recaptured as ordinary income up to the amount of the special depreciation allowance previously allowed or allowable. [Pub 946 (2025), p. 26]
- For figuring and reporting gain/loss on the sale of rental property, Pub 527 refers to **Pub 544** (and Pub 523 if it was your main home). [Pub 527 (2025), p. 2]
- **Unrecaptured §1250 gain taxed at a maximum 25% rate:** I cannot verify this — check IRS.gov. None of these three sources (Pub 527, Pub 946, Form 4562 instructions) state the 25% maximum rate on unrecaptured §1250 gain; see Pub 544 and the Schedule D instructions (Unrecaptured Section 1250 Gain Worksheet). Pub 946 does identify residential rental and nonresidential real property as **section 1250 property**. [Pub 946 (2025), p. 30]

## Georgia Notes

Georgia has NOT adopted IRC §168(k) bonus depreciation (30%/50%/100%). If bonus depreciation is claimed federally on rental assets (e.g., appliances or improvements), a Georgia Schedule 1 ADDITION is required, and separate GA depreciation schedules must be maintained for those assets in later years (GA allows the regular MACRS deduction instead). Section 179 is adopted at the increased limits but NOT for certain real property. *(Source: georgia-500-guide.md, Key Structural Facts; GA DOR, Income Tax Federal Tax Changes)*

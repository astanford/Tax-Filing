## Rules

### What's New for 2025
- Standard mileage rate for rental activities increased to **70 cents/mile**. [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 1; Pub 527 (2025), p. 1]
- **100% bonus depreciation restored** for qualified property acquired and placed in service after **January 19, 2025**; property put into service January 1–19, 2025, or acquired before January 20, 2025, and put into service later remains subject to the prior-law phase-down. [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 1; Pub 527 (2025), p. 1]
- Maximum section 179 deduction is **$2,500,000**, reduced when §179 property placed in service exceeds **$4,000,000**. [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 1; Pub 527 (2025), p. 1]
- Business interest expense limitation: for tax years beginning in 2025, adjusted taxable income adds back depreciation/amortization/depletion when determining whether interest is limited (Form 8990). [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 1]

### Schedule E vs. Schedule C (critical for short-term rentals)
- Use Schedule E Part I to report "income and expenses from rental real estate (including personal property leased with real estate)." [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 4]
- "If you rent buildings, rooms, or apartments, and provide basic services such as heat and light, trash collection, etc., you normally report your rental income and expenses on Schedule E, Part I." [Pub 527 (2025), p. 18]
- **Significant/substantial services → Schedule C.** "Generally, rental real estate activity is reported on Schedule E even if it is also a trade or business activity; however, if you provided significant services to the renter, such as maid service, report the rental activity on Schedule C, not on Schedule E. Significant services do not include the furnishing of heat and light, cleaning of public areas, trash collection, or similar services." [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 5, Line 3]
- "If you provide substantial services that are primarily for your tenant's convenience, such as regular cleaning, changing linen, or maid service, you report your rental income and expenses on Schedule C. ... Also, you may have to pay self-employment tax on your rental income using Schedule SE (Form 1040), Self-Employment Tax." [Pub 527 (2025), p. 18]
- Rental real estate income reported on Schedule E "is generally not included in net earnings from self-employment subject to self-employment tax and is generally subject to passive loss limitation rules." [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 2]
- A dwelling unit "doesn't include property (or part of the property) used solely as a hotel, motel, inn, or similar establishment. Property is used solely as a hotel, motel, inn, or similar establishment if it is regularly available for occupancy by paying customers and isn't used by an owner as a home during the year." (Relevant to classifying pure short-term-rental property under the chapter 5 personal-use rules.) [Pub 527 (2025), p. 25]
- Real estate dealers (property held for sale to customers) use Schedule C, not Schedule E. Personal property rental that is a business also goes on Schedule C. [Schedule E Instructions (2025), pp. 5–6]

### Single-Member LLC (Disregarded Entity)
- "Single-member limited liability company (LLC). In most cases, a single-member domestic LLC is not treated as a separate entity for federal income tax purposes. If you are the sole member of a domestic LLC, file Schedule E (or Schedule C or F, if applicable). However, you can elect to treat a domestic LLC as a corporation. See Form 8832 for details on the election and the tax treatment of a foreign LLC." [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 2]
- Practical effect: rentals held in a non-electing single-member domestic LLC are reported directly on the owner's Schedule E Part I as if owned personally. [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 2]
- Note: a business owned by spouses **through an LLC** (state law entity) does **not** qualify for the Qualified Joint Venture election — only businesses owned and operated by spouses as co-owners in their own names qualify. [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 2]

### Lines A and B (information-return questions)
- **Line A:** Check "Yes" if you made payments in 2025 requiring Forms 1099 — in general, $600+ paid for services to a non-employee requires Form 1099-NEC, and you must generally file Form 1099-MISC "if you paid at least $600 in rents, prizes, medical and health care payments, or other miscellaneous amounts that would be income to the person receiving them." [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 4]

### Line-by-Line: Part I

#### Lines 1a, 1b, 2 — Property, type code, days
- **Line 1a:** For each rental real estate property, show the street address, city or town, state, and ZIP code (foreign properties: city, province or state, country, postal code). [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 5]
- **Line 1b:** "Enter one of the codes listed under 'Type of Property' in Part I of the form." The instructions themselves define only these codes: code **"5"** for rental of land; code **"6"** for royalty property (leave lines 1a and 2 blank); code **"7"** for self-rental (renting to a trade or business in which you materially participated); code **"8"** for other (attach a statement describing the property). [Schedule E Instructions (2025), pp. 4–5]
  - The full code list printed on the 2025 form (Part I, "Type of Property"): **1** Single Family Residence; **2** Multi-Family Residence; **3** Vacation/Short-Term Rental; **4** Commercial; **5** Land; **6** Royalties; **7** Self-Rental; **8** Other (describe). [Schedule E (Form 1040) 2025, Part I; local copy at `reference/Raw/f1040se.pdf`]
- **Line 2:** "For each property listed on line 1a, report the number of days in the year each property was rented at fair rental value and the number of days of personal use." [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 5]
  - A day of **personal use** is any day (or part of a day) the unit was used by: you for personal purposes; any other person for personal purposes if that person owns part of the unit (unless rented under a shared-equity financing agreement); anyone in your family (or the family of another part-owner), unless the unit is rented at a fair rental price to that person as their main home; anyone who pays less than a fair rental price; or anyone under an arrangement that lets you use some other unit. [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 5]
  - **Not** counted as personal use: any day spent working substantially full time repairing and maintaining the unit (even if family used it recreationally that day); days the unit was used as your main home before or after renting it, if rented or attempted to rent for at least 12 consecutive months (or a shorter period ending in sale or exchange). [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 5]
  - **QJV checkbox:** Check the "QJV" box on line 2 for each property that is part of a qualified joint venture between spouses who each materially participate and file jointly; income, expenses, and the property must be split between spouses per their interests (each property on its own line 1/3–22). QJV income is reported "not subject to self-employment tax." [Schedule E Instructions (2025), pp. 2, 5]

#### Line 3 — Rents received
- Report rental income from real estate (including personal property leased with real estate) on line 3, one column (A, B, or C) per property. Include income for renting a room or other space. [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 5]
- If you received services or property instead of money as rent, report the **fair market value** of the services or property as rental income on line 3. [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 5]
- Rental income includes: advance rent (taxable in year received), payments for canceling a lease, expenses paid by the tenant on your behalf, and kept security deposits. Security deposits you plan to return are **not** income; a deposit that is really a final rent payment is advance rent. [Pub 527 (2025), pp. 3–4]

#### Lines 5–19 — Expenses (one column per property)
You can deduct all "ordinary and necessary expenses, such as taxes, interest, repairs, insurance, management fees, agents' commissions, and depreciation." Do not deduct the value of your own labor or amounts paid for capital investments or capital improvements. If you rent out only part of your home or other property, deduct only the part of expenses that applies to the rented part. [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 6]

Pub 527 lists the most common rental expenses: advertising; auto and travel; cleaning and maintenance; commissions; depreciation; insurance; interest (other); legal and other professional fees; local transportation; management fees; mortgage interest paid to banks, etc.; points; rental payments; repairs; taxes; utilities. [Pub 527 (2025), p. 4]

#### Lines 20–22 — Totals and loss limits per property
- **Line 20:** Total expenses. Enter total expenses for mortgage interest (line 12), depreciation/depletion (line 18), and total expenses (line 20) on lines 23c through 23e even if you have only one property. [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 6]
- **Line 21:** Income or (loss) per property. If you have amounts not at risk, use Form 6198 to determine the deductible loss, enter it on line 21, and write "Form 6198" to the left of line 21 (attach Form 6198). [Schedule E Instructions (2025), pp. 7–8]
- **Line 22:** Deductible rental real estate loss after limitations. Do not complete line 22 for royalty amounts. If the rental loss is from a passive activity, the loss may be limited — complete Form 8582 to figure the amount, if any, to enter on line 22. If the loss is not from a passive activity or you meet the "exception for certain rental real estate activities," enter the line 21 loss on line 22 without Form 8582. Prior-year unallowed losses allowed this year after Form 8582 are included on line 22. [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 8]
- **$25,000 special allowance (active participation):** If you (or your spouse) actively participated, you may deduct up to **$25,000** of rental loss against nonpassive income ($12,500 MFS living apart all year; no allowance for MFS living together). Full allowance applies with MAGI of $100,000 or less ($50,000 MFS); above that, the allowance is 50% of the difference between $150,000 ($75,000 MFS) and MAGI; generally no allowance at MAGI of $150,000+ ($75,000+ MFS). Active participation requires at least a 10% ownership interest and bona fide management decisions (approving tenants, rental terms, expenditures). [Pub 527 (2025), p. 20; Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 4]
- **Form 8582 not required** if: rental real estate (with active participation) is your only passive activity, no prior-year unallowed losses or credits, overall net loss ≤ $25,000 ($12,500 MFS living apart), MAGI ≤ $100,000 ($50,000 MFS living apart), and no limited-partner/estate-beneficiary interests. [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 4; Pub 527 (2025), pp. 20–21]
- Losses may also be subject to the excess business loss limitation (Form 461) — any disallowed amount is not reflected on Schedule E; it becomes income on Schedule 1, line 8p, carried forward as an NOL. [Schedule E Instructions (2025), pp. 1, 3]

#### Lines 23a–26 — Totals
- If you have more than three rental/royalty properties, attach as many Schedules E as needed, but fill in lines 23a through 26 on **only one** Schedule E, as the combined totals for all properties. [Schedule E Instructions (2025), pp. 2, 4]
- On lines 23a–23e enter the applicable totals (23c = total of line 12, 23d = total of line 18, 23e = total of line 20). [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 6; Pub 527 (2025), p. 21]
- Lines 24–26 (income, losses, total): the line 26 total carries to Part V, line 41 ("Total income or (loss)") and from there to the Form 1040. [Pub 527 (2025), p. 18 ("include the amount from line 26 (Part I) in the 'Total income or (loss)' on line 41 (Part V)")]

### 1099-MISC / 1099-K Connection
- **Payer side (Line A):** You must generally file Form 1099-MISC if you paid at least $600 in rents, and Form 1099-NEC if you paid $600+ for services to a non-employee. [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 4]
- **Royalties:** If you received $10 or more in royalties, the payer should send a Form 1099-MISC or similar statement by January 31, 2026; report on line 4. [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 6]
- **Receiving a 1099-MISC Box 1 (rents) for rent collected on your behalf (e.g., by a property manager), or a Form 1099-K for platform payouts (Airbnb/VRBO):** neither PDF addresses these information returns for rent recipients. I cannot verify this — check IRS.gov. Regardless of any information return, all rents received belong on line 3 (cash-basis taxpayers report income in the year actually or constructively received, e.g., when credited to your account). [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 5; Pub 527 (2025), p. 3]

### Personal Use / Vacation Home Rules (§280A; Pub 527 chapter 5)
- If you have any personal use of a dwelling unit you rent, divide expenses between rental and personal use: rental expenses generally may be no more than total expenses multiplied by (days actually rented at a fair rental price ÷ total days the unit is used). Only the rental portion goes on Schedule E; some personal portions (mortgage interest, taxes) may go on Schedule A. [Pub 527 (2025), p. 25]
- **"Home" test:** "You use a dwelling unit as a home during the tax year if you use it for personal purposes more than the greater of: (1) 14 days, or (2) 10% of the total days it is rented to others at a fair rental price." [Pub 527 (2025), p. 26; Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 5]
- A day rented at a fair rental price counts as a rental-use day for dividing expenses even if you used it personally that day — but counts as a personal day for the "home" test. Days available for rent but not actually rented are NOT rental days. [Pub 527 (2025), p. 26]
- **Fair rental price:** "The rent you charge isn't a fair rental price if it is substantially less than the rents charged for other properties that are similar to your property in your area" (compare purpose, size, condition, furnishings, location). [Pub 527 (2025), p. 26]
- Personal-use days include use by family members (unless it's their main home rented at fair rental price), anyone paying less than a fair rental price, co-owners, and reciprocal-use arrangements; days spent substantially full time on repairs and maintenance don't count. [Pub 527 (2025), pp. 26–27; Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 5]
- **Used as a home but rented fewer than 15 days:** "its primary function isn't considered to be rental and it shouldn't be reported on Schedule E (Form 1040). You aren't required to report the rental income and rental expenses from this activity." Mortgage interest, property taxes, and qualified casualty losses are reported as normally allowed on Schedule A. [Pub 527 (2025), p. 28; Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 5]
- **Used as a home and rented 15 days or more:** include all rental income; divide expenses; if there is a net loss, the deduction for certain rental expenses is limited (rental expenses beyond gross rental income generally can't offset other income) — use Worksheet 5-1; disallowed amounts carry forward as rental expenses for the same property. Renting a dwelling unit considered a home isn't a passive activity (the §280A limit applies instead). [Pub 527 (2025), p. 28; Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 5]
- **Not used as a home:** deduct all rental-portion expenses, subject to the at-risk and passive activity loss rules; deductible rental expenses can exceed gross rental income. [Pub 527 (2025), p. 28; Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 5]
- Caution from the instructions: expenses related to personal-use days never qualify as rental expenses — e.g., 7 personal days and 63 rental days means in most cases 10% (7 ÷ 70) of expenses are not rental expenses. [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 5]

### Rented Below Fair Rental Value / Not Rented for Profit
- Renting at less than a fair rental price makes those days **personal-use days** (e.g., renting an apartment to your mother at less than fair rental price = personal use). [Pub 527 (2025), pp. 26–27]
- **Not rented for profit:** you must still report the rental income, but you can no longer deduct rental expenses. Report not-for-profit rental income on Schedule 1 (Form 1040), line 8j — not Schedule E. Mortgage interest (main/second home), real estate taxes, and casualty losses may still be deductible on Schedule A if you itemize. [Pub 527 (2025), p. 24]
- **Presumption of profit:** rental income exceeding expenses in at least 3 of 5 consecutive years presumes a for-profit rental (Form 5213 to postpone the determination). [Pub 527 (2025), p. 24]

### Repairs vs. Improvements
- "You must capitalize any expense you pay to improve your rental property. An expense is for an improvement if it results in a betterment to your property, restores your property, or adapts your property to a new or different use." [Pub 527 (2025), p. 7]
- Repairs/maintenance keep the property in an ordinarily efficient operating condition (fixing a broken lock, painting a room) and are deducted on line 14; improvements (e.g., adding substantial insulation, replacing an entire HVAC system) "must generally be capitalized and depreciated (that is, they cannot be deducted in full in the year they are paid or incurred)." [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 7]
- Examples of improvements (Table 1-1): additions (bedroom, bathroom, deck, garage, porch, patio); lawn & grounds (landscaping, driveway, walkway, fence, retaining wall, sprinkler system, swimming pool); miscellaneous (storm windows/doors, new roof, central vacuum, wiring upgrades, satellite dish, security system); heating & A/C (heating system, central air conditioning, furnace, duct work, central humidifier, filtration system); plumbing (septic system, water heater, soft water system, filtration system); interior (built-in appliances, kitchen modernization, flooring, wall-to-wall carpeting); insulation (attic, walls, floor, pipes, duct work). [Pub 527 (2025), p. 8, Table 1-1]
- Improvements are depreciated as separate property, using the recovery period of the underlying property as if placed in service at the same time (e.g., an addition to a residential rental = 27.5 years). [Pub 527 (2025), pp. 13–14]
- **De minimis safe harbor for tangible property:** "If you elect this de minimis safe harbor for your rental activity for the tax year, you aren't required to capitalize the de minimis costs of acquiring or producing certain real and tangible personal property and may deduct these amounts as rental expenses on line 19 of Schedule E." [Pub 527 (2025), p. 7] The commonly cited **$2,500-per-invoice/item ceiling** is not stated in either PDF — I cannot verify this — check IRS.gov (Tangible Property Regulations FAQs, referenced at Pub 527 (2025), p. 7).
- **Routine maintenance safe harbor:** a cost that would otherwise be an improvement to a building or equipment may still be deductible under the routine maintenance safe harbor (see Tangible Property Regulations FAQs). [Pub 527 (2025), p. 7]
- Keep the costs of repairs and improvements separate — you will need improvement costs when you sell or depreciate the property. [Pub 527 (2025), p. 7]

### Depreciation Basics (cross-reference)
- Depreciation begins when the property is **ready and available** for rent ("placed in service"), not when first actually rented; it continues while the property is temporarily idle (e.g., between tenants). [Pub 527 (2025), pp. 9, "Placed in Service" and "Idle Property"]
- Property converted from personal to rental use: depreciable basis is the **lesser of FMV or adjusted basis** on the conversion date. [Pub 527 (2025), p. 23]
- Land is never depreciable; allocate purchase price between land and building by relative FMV or assessed values. [Schedule E Instructions (2025), p. 7; Pub 527 (2025), pp. 9, 11]
- See `reference/Raw/i4562.pdf` and Pub 946 (`reference/Raw/p946.pdf`) for computation mechanics; all math via Python per CLAUDE.md.

## Georgia Notes

- Net rental income/loss flows into federal AGI and therefore into GA Form 500 Line 8 — Georgia has no separate rental schedule. *(Source: georgia-500-guide.md, Line 8)*
- Georgia has NOT adopted IRC §168(k) bonus depreciation — if bonus was claimed federally on rental assets, a GA Schedule 1 addition is required. *(Source: georgia-500-guide.md, Key Structural Facts)*
